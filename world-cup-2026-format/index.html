World Cup 2026 Format

The 2026 FIFA World Cup is the first men's edition to expand to 48 teams, and it will be staged across three host countries and 16 host cities. That change makes the tournament bigger in scale, longer in rhythm, and more layered in the way readers will follow teams, players, and knockout paths.

48 teams

The first men's World Cup with a 48-team field

World Cup 2026 expands the field from 32 teams to 48, making it the largest men's tournament in the competition's history. That single change alters the scale of coverage across the whole site, because more national teams, more player profiles, and more group-stage storylines can coexist without feeling secondary.

For an editorial project, that matters. A bigger field means a stronger need for structured hubs, clear entity pages, and navigational routes between teams, players, and tournament context.

16 host cities

A tournament spread across 16 cities

The tournament will be played across 16 host cities in North America. FIFA lists Atlanta, Boston, Dallas, Guadalajara, Houston, Kansas City, Los Angeles, Mexico City, Miami, Monterrey, New York New Jersey, Philadelphia, Seattle, San Francisco Bay Area, Toronto, and Vancouver as the host cities.

That geographic spread gives the tournament a different editorial feel from more compact editions. Host cities are not just background detail here; they are part of the experience layer and deserve direct internal links from the main format page.

Host countries

Three countries, one tournament frame

World Cup 2026 will be co-hosted by the United States, Mexico, and Canada, making it the first men's World Cup held across three host countries. The three host nations also qualify automatically as tournament hosts.

This matters structurally as well as symbolically. A three-country tournament changes travel, atmosphere, regional framing, and how readers think about the event before a ball is even kicked.

Tournament structure

12 groups, more knockout access, and a new round

The 48 teams will be divided into 12 groups of four. Each team plays three group-stage matches, the top two teams in each group advance, and the eight best third-place teams also move on.

That creates a Round of 32 before the usual Round of 16, quarter-finals, semi-finals, and final. The expanded format also increases the total number of matches to 104, and the eventual champion will need to play eight games instead of seven.
